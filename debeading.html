Introduction

What is FEA? and What is it used for in this project?

Engineers always try to come up with mathematical models that let you analyze and predict the future of a physical system from some differential equations. However, these equations can become too many to solve the problem by hand. So FEA was created to solve this issue. In a nutshell, FEA is a numerical method that lets you break down a physical situation into many algebraic equations. This method is typically performed in computers because the number of equations that come out of this are usually too many to do by hand (even in many years).
For this project, FEA was used to model a tire debeading. Which for those of you who do not know (I didn't when I first started this project), debeading is the action of separating the tire from the rim of the vehicle. An example of this is shown in the image below.

Technical Knowledge For FEA

The FEA method can be performed by many computer softwares, but for this one, the software at hand will be LS-Dyna. Some interesting characteristics for LS-Dyna is the fact that it uses Fortran compilers to work. Thus you have to manually code any features you add to your model. This is rare in many actual softwares but the accuracy that LS-Dyna has is still remarkable for costly computational models.

Basic Steps for Modeling in FEA

Step 0: Create A Model
-Usually this can be a step that you do not necessarily do because in many jobs, the models are provided to you. However, if you are starting from scratch a model, you should be free to create it from any modeling software that does 3D drawing (such as SolidWords or Autocad).
Step 1:Create a Mesh
-Now, here is where it becomes harder. Meshing is the technique of creating small "elements" together as shown below. These elements is where the calculations take place. Imagine that you want to calculate the velocity of a rod as it vibrates. If you represent that rod with 4 points connected, then you will have a somewhat "model" of the rod. You will be able to perform math on each of those 4 points, but you will realize that using more points provides more accuracy and takes longer. Now if we make more points, lets say 8 in total, and connect them together, we will have many "square" elements together representing the rod. This again, will increase accuracy (because the more points you have, the more it resembles a "real" rod) and take longer to compute. There exists many ways in which the points can be connected to form "elements", and the accumulation of all these elements is known as a Mesh. Some examples of element types are shown in the Figure below. Meshing is probably the hardest part in FEA given that depending how the mesh is created, the model could take many hours, days or weeks to run! In industry time equals money, so a long simulation will probably be a reduction in salary. But it is not only about time, but accuracy as well!
Step 2:Define Material Properties
-If meshing is performed correctly, choosing material properties is usually a simpler task. Depending on the model you are making (in my case it was a tire), you would need to select materials such as steel for the rims or rubber for the treadwall, to make your model realistic. The importance of this comes from empirical data. Material models need to be accurate, but you are the ones giving many of the properties. Some examples include Young's Modulus, Poisson's Ratio, Ultimate Tensile Strength, and more.
Step 3: Define Initial Conditions/Constraints
-Now that we have everything else, the mission is to program some initial conditions or constraints that the physical system has. For example, if you want to see a tire rotate, you would add a rotational velocity along with a forward velocity to the center of mass of the tire. This way you will see the movement of the tire, and everything else that happens after, is what you need to analyze as an engineer.
Step 4: Simulation Analysis
-Often this step is overlooked, but many people believe that just because a simulation runs, it means that it is correct. WRONG. Never assume that simulations are correct until you throughly analyze data and draw conclusions that have logical arguments behind them. If you do a simulation of a car crashing into a wall, and you see the that "it crashes" as expected, but then look into the data files to find that the energy stored in many elements exceded 1 trillion Joules of energy. Then it means your simulation looks pretty, but it also means that a car crash has more energy than many nuclear weapons out there. This step usually involves answering the questions: "Is my data correct?", "How is it correct?", and if it is "What does this help me to know?" Example: If your simulation of a car crash is correct, you have an idea of how much energy can a vehicle absorb. This means that you can design a vehicle to resist many types of impact scenarios.

Tire Debeading Project

This project will be presented in the same format as the steps I showed in the introduction to offer a "consistent" flow of thought. However, some knowledge about mechanical properties and FEA detailed aspects is assumed from the reader. One of the main reasons for choosing a tire debading is because there is not a lot of research about tire behavior when vehicles hit the curb at high speeds. Curb impact provides lateral forces in the tires which are something not usually taken into account when designing the tire. Therefore this project can serve as a guide to understanding the resistance of tires to lateral impact for future design.

Step 1: Tire Meshing

The model used consisted of the following components:

  • Outer Rim: Modeled with Shell elements and element Belytscho-Tsay formulation
  • Inner Rim: Modeled with Shell elements and same element formulation, constrained to the outer rim with a failure mode to produce debeading upon impact.
  • Shaft: Modeled with Shell elements and Belytscho-Tsay element formulation, this part is fully constrained to the inner rim.
  • Tread: Shell elements with Bathe-Dvorkin formulations were used. Given the tread had the highest deformation, a more accurate element formulation was used. High element thickness was used to avoid instabilities caused on the rubber during lateral impact.
  • Sidewall: Same parameters as the tread. Special consideration was made on the sidewall meshing given that to simulate air in the tire, the sidewall has to form an enclosed volume.
  • Curb: To save time on modeling the curve, automatic meshing was used. Material was defined rigid, and was fixed to the ground. Special considerations included avoiding sharp edges.

Overall, the picture below summarizes the model components. Standard dimensions of tire were P215/55R17 taken from a Nissan Altima model.

Suspension Considerations

One of the most important parts for modeling tire behavior is the suspension model. For our purposes, just the main parts of the suspension are taken into account. These are the Control Arm, Tie Rod, and the Spring/Strut assembly. Mathematically, a mass-spring-damper systems offer simplifications for the suspension. To model this, a control arm was modeled with rigid propertie s, and a discrete element was used to represent the total effect of the spring and the strut on the suspension as shown in the Figure below. To obtain a more realistic model, suspension design needs to be implemented. As of now, this would serve as an idealized suspension model.

Step 2: Materials Overview for Tire Components

When choosing materials for an FEA model, there is a question that needs to be always asked: Is the part going to deform or not? If the answer is not, the modeling your part as a solid part will incredibly reduce the amount of work you will have to do. This occurs because solid parts often do not require much computation time (since they are rigid) and will be easy to mesh as well. If the parts are indeed a deformable part, then a lot of time needs to be devoted to create a fine mesh and making sure that material properties are selected carefully. We will follow a similar format as in the meshing for the materials:

  • Outer Rim: This part was modeled with steel properties and as a rigid material.
  • Inner Rim: This part was modeled with steel properties and as a rigid material.
  • Shaft: This part was modeled with steel properties and as a rigid material.
  • Tread: This part was modeled with rubber properties and as a deformable material.
  • Sidewall: This part was modeled with rubber properties and as a deformable material .
  • Curb: This part was modeled with concrete properties and as a rigid material.

When defining a material in FEA, it is important to always include the must fundamental properties of any material. These properties are the Modulus of Elasticity, Mass Density, and the Poisson's Ratio

As we said before, modeling the rigid parts is easy, but the deformable ones are not. Even more complicated, we have a rubber deformable material. Rubber is known to be one of the most impredictable materials in existance, and predicting its behavior mathematically has been one of the highest challenges I have seen in FEA modeling. For this reason I was not able to just simply "choose" any rubber parameters. I had to investigate which rubber model would be useful to my simulation. Why?

Well, there are types of material that are isotropic or anisotropic. Isotropic are material that exhibit the same behavior regardless of where you apply your forces. And, anisotropic materials are those that behave differently depending on where you apply the forces. Usually, rubber is believed to be isotropic, but research has developed other types of rubber that tend to be anisotropic. So guess what, a different behavior means a different type of model. Is the rubber of my tire isotropic or anistropic? We'll see. For now let's bring another point. Rubber is a hyperelasatic material, what does that even mean? Well, in Materials Science, there is what is called the Strain-Stress Curve which is able to describe the deforming behavior of many materials. Hyperelastic Materials have this Strain-Stress Curve dervied from a Strain Energy Density Function. What does that mean again? It means that the behavior of this material can be totally modified depending on how this Function is defined. This is bad news because Rubber is a Hyperelastic Material. So there are a lot (and I mean a lot) of mathematical models to describe many behaviors of rubber.

One of the easiest ways to choose the behavior you want is to physically test the rubber piece you want to use and see its properties. But that is not the case with me because I do not have any rubber nor testing equipment at this moment. So what did I do? I chose different Hyperelastic Models and simulate them to see which one has the more realistic behavior.
